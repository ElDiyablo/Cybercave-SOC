// Ticketing

Incident Ticket Workflow

Documentation is the most important thing an analyst produces. A great ticket turns an observation into an actionable record — and it's what separates professional analysts from amateurs.

// 01 — Why It Matters

Why Good Tickets Matter

When you detect a potential security event, your ticket is the single source of truth for everyone who touches that case after you — the next analyst on shift, the SOC lead, the instructor, or eventually a responder taking action. A bad ticket wastes everyone's time. A good ticket accelerates the response, builds organizational trust, and creates a paper trail that can be reviewed, learned from, and used in after-action reporting.

📎

Evidence Chain

A ticket preserves exactly what you saw, when you saw it, and what you did. This chain of evidence is critical if the event escalates.

🤝

Analyst Handoff

Shifts change. A well-written ticket means the next person can pick up exactly where you left off without re-investigating from scratch.

📈

Program Improvement

Aggregated ticket data shows where threats are coming from and how analysts are developing — the raw material for improving the SOC.

// 02 — Ticket Anatomy

What Goes in a Ticket

Every ticket should cover these six elements — think of them as the journalistic standard applied to security: who, what, when, where, how, and so what.

Title & Classification

A concise one-line description including the threat category if known (e.g., "Suspicious outbound C2 traffic — 192.168.1.45 → 45.33.32.156"). Classify severity: Low / Medium / High / Critical.

Event Summary

Two to four sentences describing what happened in plain language. Write for someone who hasn't seen the logs. What triggered your attention? What does the behavior suggest?

Timeline & Observables

Key events with timestamps. Include source IP, destination IP, protocol, port, action (allowed/denied), and threat category labels from the PA log.

# Example observable format
2024-07-15 14:32:01 UTC src=10.0.1.22 dst=185.220.101.5 proto=TCP dport=443 action=allow threat=C2
2024-07-15 14:32:08 UTC src=10.0.1.22 dst=185.220.101.5 proto=TCP dport=443 action=allow threat=C2
# Repeated beacon pattern — 7-second interval

Indicator Enrichment

Document OTX results, VirusTotal scores, AbuseIPDB reports, or WHOIS data for the IPs/domains/hashes involved. State clearly: known bad, or unknown/unverified?

Analysis & MITRE Mapping

What does this behavior mean? Map to a MITRE ATT&CK technique if possible (e.g., T1071.001 — Application Layer Protocol: Web Protocols). Note your confidence level.

Recommended Action

What should happen next? Block the IP? Escalate to SOC lead? Continue monitoring? False positive — no action? Be explicit. This drives the response.

// 05 — Common Mistakes

What to Avoid

MistakeWhy It's a ProblemBetter Approach
Vague title "Suspicious activity" tells the next analyst nothing. Be specific: "Port scan from 10.0.1.88 targeting 22/23/80"
No timestamps Without timestamps, you can't reconstruct the event order. Always include UTC timestamps for every log entry cited.
Logs with no context A wall of raw log lines without explanation is not an analysis. Summarize what the logs show first, then paste supporting evidence.
Skipping enrichment Flagging an IP without checking its reputation is incomplete triage. Always check OTX/VirusTotal/AbuseIPDB before closing the investigation.
No recommended action A ticket that doesn't say "what next" creates bottlenecks. End every ticket with a clear recommendation, even if it's "no action — FP".
