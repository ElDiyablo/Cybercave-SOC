// 01 — Phishing

How to Spot a Phish

Phishing is the #1 attack vector against universities. Attackers impersonate IT departments, financial aid offices, professors, and popular services to steal credentials or install malware.

🚨 If you clicked a suspicious link or entered credentials: Change your password immediately and report it to the SOC using the incident form. Don't wait.

Red Flags to Watch For

📧

Suspicious Sender

The display name says "IT Help Desk" but the actual email address is support@random-domain.com. Always check the full address, not just the name.

⚡

Urgent / Threatening Language

"Your account will be deleted in 24 hours." Urgency is designed to short-circuit your critical thinking. Slow down.

🔗

Suspicious Links

Hover over any link before clicking. Watch for subtle misspellings: msu-edu.login.com is not your university.

📎

Unexpected Attachments

Unexpected Word docs, PDFs, or ZIP files — especially from unknown senders. Malware is frequently delivered as "invoices" or "shared documents."

🔑

Credential Requests

Legitimate university IT will never ask for your password via email. Ever. If a page is asking for your login after clicking an email link, close it immediately.

🎁

Too Good to Be True

"You've been selected for a $500 gift card." These are especially common during financial aid season. If it sounds too good — it's a lure.

Common Campus Phishing Themes

Lure TypeWhat It ClaimsWhat It Actually Does
Financial Aid Phish"Your FAFSA needs verification — log in now"Steals university login credentials
IT Helpdesk Phish"Your email storage is full — click to expand"Harvests email credentials or installs malware
Professor Impersonation"Hey, I need you to buy gift cards for class supplies"Gift card fraud / financial theft
Job Offer Scam"Easy remote job — $25/hr, just fill out this form"Identity theft / check cashing fraud
Package Notification"Your package couldn't be delivered — verify address"Credential theft / credit card fraud

When in doubt, don't click. Go directly to the official website by typing the URL manually. Report suspicious emails to the SOC.

// 02 — Passwords

Password Management

Weak and reused passwords are responsible for a massive percentage of account takeovers. Here's how to fix that permanently.

🔐

Use a Password Manager

Generates and stores a unique, complex password for every account. You only remember one strong master password. This is the single highest-impact thing you can do for your security.

Recommended: Bitwarden (free), 1Password, KeePassXC

📱

Enable MFA Everywhere

Multi-factor authentication means an attacker needs your password AND your phone. Enable it on your university account, email, and any important service. Use an authenticator app over SMS.
